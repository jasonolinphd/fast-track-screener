Am I a Candidate for the ADHD Fast Track?

Five questions to help clarify your situation. This is educational, not a clinical determination.

Question 1 of 5
Which best describes where you are right now?
This helps tailor the information to your specific situation.
Question 2 of 5
When was the last date you took an ADHD medication, meaning the last pill or tablet you actually consumed?
Select the year that best applies. If you are unsure, choose the most recent possible year.
Question 3 of 5
Besides ADHD, have you ever been diagnosed with any neurological or mental health condition?
Include past diagnoses, even if you consider them resolved.
Question 4 of 5
Over the past four years, how would you describe your work or academic performance and your daily functioning overall?
The FAA looks for evidence that ADHD is in remission, not just that medication stopped.
Question 5 of 5
Do you have access to pharmacy records covering the past four years?
The FAA requires pharmacy records as part of the Fast Track documentation package. This includes records showing no fills, not just records of fills.

Deferred but Still on Track
A deferral does not mean denial. Fast Track is likely still available to you.

When an AME defers your application, the FAA takes over the review timeline. That said, if you meet the Fast Track criteria, the pathway is still the same. The key difference is that the documentation package now needs to be a direct response to the FAA's request.

This is where working with an evaluator familiar with FAA expectations makes a real difference. An incomplete or misformatted submission at this stage adds months to the process.

Recommended next steps
Do not schedule any additional AME appointments before completing the Fast Track evaluation
Gather pharmacy records, academic records, and your personal statement now
Bring a copy of the FAA deferral letter to the consultation

Wait for the FAA Letter
The right next step is to wait for communication from the FAA before doing anything.

Once an AME submits a deferral, the FAA takes over the review. Acting before receiving their guidance, including scheduling an evaluation, can create confusion and may not address what the FAA actually needs.

When the letter arrives, it will specify what type of evaluation or documentation is required. At that point the pathway becomes clear and moving quickly will matter. Having your records organized now is a good use of the waiting period.

Useful things to do while you wait
Pull your pharmacy records covering the past four years
Gather academic transcripts, prior evaluations, and any relevant medical records
Review the FAA ADHD Fast Track overview so you understand the process before the letter arrives
Once you receive the FAA letter, a consultation will help clarify whether Fast Track or Standard Track applies to your situation and what to do next.
